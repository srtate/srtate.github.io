NPC proofs

This write-up is an attempt to help guide you in creating NP completeness proofs after you learn the basics. You should read Chapter 7 in the textbook first, which introduces the ideas from the ground up, and then you can go through this. The organization here is different: I start with what you want to do (write a proof that some language is NP complete) and work backwards to uncover the pieces that have to be in the proof. Since I’m working from the end backwards, it’s a terrible way to try to initialy learn the concepts. However, once you understand the basics, this is a good way of thinking about how to apply those concepts.

I’ll first guide you through the structure of an NP-completeness proof alongside a complete NP-completeness proof, so you can see the general idea and motivation for the structure, and then a concrete realization of that in a proof. The concrete proof is a proof that a language called \(\textit{PARTITION}\) is NP-complete.

On the next page, you will find the definition of the \(\textit{PARTITION}\) problem and the full NP-completeness proof. Read the definition carefully so you understand the problem, and then read through the proof. You don’t have to understand everything in the proof at this point – we’ll walk through it piece by piece after that.

Here’s a brief “table of contents” of these pages:

  1. Full NP-completeness proof for \(\textit{PARTITION}\)

  2. Part-by-part examination of the required parts of an NP-completeness proof, indentifying specifically where they are in the example proof.

  3. Grading criteria for an NP-completeness proof. Basically, now to “grade” your own proof and make sure all the requirements are met.
